There's a specific kind of longing that comes from music you've never heard before — music that sounds like a memory of a place you've never been.

I first noticed it with a record I found at a car boot sale in East London. No label, no liner notes, just a hand-stamped sleeve that read *Room Tone* in smudged ink. I bought it for fifty pence and forgot about it for three weeks.

## The first listen

When I finally played it, something strange happened. The opening drone — a slow, wet reverb that bloomed like fog over still water — felt *familiar*. Not déjà vu exactly. More like recognising a smell from childhood that you couldn't quite name.

This is what I've started calling false memory: the sensation that a piece of music is reaching backward into a past that wasn't yours. It doesn't belong to nostalgia in the traditional sense. Nostalgia requires something *lost*. This is grief for something you never had.

## Constructed pasts

A handful of artists have made this their entire practice. The Caretaker spent nearly a decade releasing music that simulates the degradation of Alzheimer's memory — ballroom recordings eaten alive by static and time. William Basinski's *Disintegration Loops* are literally that: tape dissolving as it plays, each pass a little more ruined than the last.

What these records share is a relationship to *erasure*. The music is always already disappearing. And in that disappearance, something emotional floods in to fill the gap.

> "We construct the past the same way we construct the future — imperfectly, from available materials, with a bias toward meaning."

The records that trigger false memory tend to be lo-fi, washed out, harmonically simple. They resist precision. There's room in the blurriness for the listener to project.

## Why it works

I think it comes down to the architecture of how we store emotional experience. Memories aren't recordings — they're reconstructions, reassembled each time we access them. Music that mimics the texture of that reconstruction (hazy, incomplete, slightly wrong) can slip past our defences and feel like it belongs to us.

The *Room Tone* record still sits by my turntable. I still don't know who made it, or when, or where. But I have a whole emotional landscape attached to it now — none of which is real, all of which is mine.

That might be the most honest thing music can do.
